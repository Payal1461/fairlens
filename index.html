A note from the auditor

Your dataset leans heavily toward one group, and there's a column quietly carrying caste information. Nothing here is catastrophic — but if you trained a model today, certain people would be more likely to get a "no" before they ever got a fair look.

01 Representation

Some voices are missing

Your dataset is 72% male and 28% female. That's not a 50/50 world — but it's also not the world we live in. A model trained on this will learn what "male applicants" look like, much better than what female applicants do.

02 Hidden proxies

A column is doing something it shouldn't

Pincode shows a 0.71 correlation with religion in your data. Even if you remove the religion column, the model can guess it from the pincode. This is how bias smuggles itself in.

Pincode → Religion r = 0.71
Surname → Caste r = 0.58
School name → Income class r = 0.49

→ Drop these columns, or transform them (e.g., bucket pincodes into broader regions).

03 Outcome gap

The outcomes aren't equal

Among applicants with similar income and credit scores, men got approved 68% of the time. Women got approved 41% of the time. That's a 27-point gap the model will happily learn.

How Fairlens looks at your data

Four passes, in plain language.

1

Who's in the room?

We count every group — gender, age bracket, region — and tell you who's missing.

2

Who's whispering through proxies?

Pincode, surname, school — innocent-looking columns that secretly carry identity.

3

Are the outcomes fair?

We compare approval/rejection/selection rates across groups using the 80% rule.

4

What would help?

Each finding comes with a plain, concrete suggestion you can act on before training.

Why this matters

A model that's biased before training stays biased after.

You can tune hyperparameters all you want. You can add regularization, change architectures, run grid searches for weeks. None of it fixes a dataset that didn't include enough of the people it's going to judge.

Fairness isn't a metric you bolt on at the end. It starts with the question: who is in this data, and who isn't?
